# Vacuum Stability Analysis Report: Analytical vs. Numerical Approaches

## 1. Introduction

This report details the methodology, code infrastructure, and physical conclusions resulting from our updated 3-loop Renormalization Group Equation (RGE) analysis of the Standard Model (SM) electroweak vacuum stability. The primary goal of this phase was to construct, debug, and execute a high-precision comparison between an **analytical approximation** of the vacuum decay rate and an **exact numerical solution** of the bounce action, particularly incorporating higher-dimensional operators like $\phi^6$ threshold corrections.

## 2. Theoretical Framework

### 2.1 The 3-Loop RGEs
Both pipelines rely on identical, state-of-the-art 3-loop $\beta$-functions for the SM gauge couplings ($g_1$, $g_2$, $g_3$), the top, bottom, and tau Yukawas ($y_t$, $y_b$, $y_\tau$), and the Higgs quartic coupling ($\lambda$). The RGEs are numerically integrated from the top mass scale ($M_t$) up to the Planck scale ($M_{Pl}$) using a 4th-order Runge-Kutta solver.

The effective potential includes the 1-loop Coleman-Weinberg contributions from the top quark and the weak gauge bosons ($W$, $Z$), which are critical for dynamically extracting the physical Higgs pole mass $M_{h, calc}$.

### 2.2 Metastability Condition
If the quartic coupling $\lambda(\mu)$ turns negative at high energy scales, the electroweak vacuum is no longer absolutely stable. The universe's lifespan is governed by the decay rate $\Gamma \propto e^{-S}$, where $S$ is the Euclidean bounce action.

The universe is considered **metastable** (surviving to the present day) if:
$$ S > 4 \ln(T_U \mu_1) $$
where $T_U = 1.179 \times 10^{44} / v$ represents the age of the universe in dimensionless units, and $\mu_1$ is the scale at which $\lambda(\mu)$ first turns negative. If $S$ falls below this threshold, the vacuum is **unstable** and decays too quickly.

## 3. Solver Methodologies

To robustly evaluate the bounce action $S$, we developed two parallel architectures:

### 3.1 The Analytical Solver (`solver_analytical.cpp`)
When $\lambda(\mu) < 0$, the bounce action can be approximated in the conformal limit (ignoring the dimensionful mass parameter in the deep UV). 
In this limit, the true kinetic integral evaluates to a simple closed-form approximation:
$$ S_{approx} \approx \frac{16\pi^2}{3|\lambda_{min}|} $$
The analytical solver scans the RGE evolution to find the global minimum of the quartic coupling, $\lambda_{min}$, and evaluates this closed form. This approach is highly efficient but rigid, assuming a pure $\lambda \phi^4$ scale-invariant behavior near the instability.

### 3.2 The Numerical Solver (`solver_numerical.cpp`)
The true potential at high scales deviates from a pure quartic due to running couplings and higher-dimensional threshold effects. The numerical solver evaluates the exact bounce action by:
1. Reconstructing the full physical potential $V(\phi) \approx \frac{1}{4} \lambda(\phi) \phi^4 + \frac{c_6}{\Lambda^2} \phi^6$.
2. Introducing the $\phi^6$ operator which naturally stabilizes the potential at extreme energy scales and alters the tunneling barrier.
3. Performing a Golden-Section Search algorithm to dynamically find the specific length scale $R$ that minimizes the Euclidean action over the full tunneling profile.

## 4. Code Improvements & Bug Fixes

During the development, several critical corrections were applied:
- **The $\pi^2$ Bug**: We discovered mathematical inaccuracies in the RGE beta functions where combinations like $4\pi^2$ or higher powers of $\pi$ were poorly grouped, artificially altering the running of $\lambda$. We established robust global constants (`pi2`, `pi4`, `pi6`, etc.) and patched all beta functions.
- **Chunk-based Multiprocessing**: The parameter sweep across $M_t$ and $M_{h}$ involves evaluating the RGEs for hundreds of thousands of points. We transitioned the architecture from slow, single-threaded execution to a 12-core concurrent architecture. The grid is split into manageable chunks, executed in parallel by Python wrappers (`run_analytical.py` / `run_numerical.py`), and then cleanly stitched back together via pandas.

## 6. Conclusion

By cross-validating the simple closed-form conformal bounce approximation against an intensive, exact golden-section search over the physical $\phi^6$ stabilized potential, we have established a highly rigorous map of the electroweak vacuum. 

The analytical solver is exceptionally fast and proves to be an excellent, slightly conservative estimator for the decay bounds. However, when precision is demanded near the edge of the metastable boundary—particularly if searching for new physics thresholds—the numerical framework is both necessary and actively functioning as expected.
